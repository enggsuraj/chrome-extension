Why your eyes deserve more than a quick break

By Maria Chen — published April 28, 2026

The average knowledge worker spends more than nine hours a day looking at a screen. That's more time than most of us spend asleep, and it shows: dry eyes, blurred vision, and the strange "afterimage" you get when you finally walk outside.

For decades, optometrists have recommended a simple rhythm — every twenty minutes, look at something twenty feet away for twenty seconds. The science behind it is surprisingly elegant.

"It's not the screen itself that's harmful. It's the lack of distance variety in our visual diet."

When your gaze locks at a single distance for too long, the small ciliary muscles inside your eye stay contracted. They were designed to flex throughout the day, just like any other muscle. The "20-20-20 rule" is, in essence, a stretching routine for your eyes.
